# Gregor Mendel: The garden knows the ratios

## Use in the game

This is a historically inspired path outline for a Reigns-style science career game. It should guide card writing, not serve as a strict biography. The player should feel the pressure of balancing **Credibility, Curiosity, Cash, and Capacity**.

## Source notes for core facts

- Britannica overview: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Gregor-Mendel
- Mendel Museum: https://mendelmuseum.muni.cz/en/
- Cold Spring Harbor DNA Learning Center Mendel: https://dnalc.cshl.edu/view/16182-Gregor-Mendel-1822-1884-.html

## Core factual anchors

- Mendel used pea plant crosses to describe basic principles of heredity.
- His work was published in the 1860s but became widely recognized later.
- Safe satire zones include counting peas, patient experiments, ignored talks, and the mismatch between clear data and a field not ready for the idea.

## Recurring motifs

- The peas are more organized than the audience.
- A ratio can be correct and still be ignored.

## Career arc beats

1. A monastery garden becomes an experimental system.
2. Careful crosses reveal stable patterns.
3. Mathematical clarity fails to create immediate fame.
4. Administrative duties interrupt science.
5. Legacy arrives after the scientist is gone.

## Potential pathway cards

1. **The garden offers peas with clean visible traits.**
   - Choice A: Design controlled crosses
   - Choice B: Plant whatever grows
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Curiosity

2. **A colleague says counting thousands of peas sounds dull.**
   - Choice A: Count them anyway
   - Choice B: Use a smaller sample
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Capacity

3. **The ratios look too neat to be coincidence.**
   - Choice A: Trust the math
   - Choice B: Call it a curiosity
   - Main tension: Curiosity vs Care

4. **A talk audience politely fails to panic.**
   - Choice A: Explain again
   - Choice B: Abandon the argument
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Capacity

5. **A journal accepts the paper, then the world mostly shrugs.**
   - Choice A: Preserve the record
   - Choice B: Chase louder topics
   - Main tension: Care vs Cash

6. **Administrative work takes over the week.**
   - Choice A: Protect experiment time
   - Choice B: Serve the institution
   - Main tension: Curiosity vs Credibility

7. **A plant line does not behave as expected.**
   - Choice A: Check the trait carefully
   - Choice B: Hide the exception
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Reputation

8. **You need more generations before claiming a pattern.**
   - Choice A: Wait and cross again
   - Choice B: Publish the early ratio
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Curiosity

9. **The vocabulary for genes does not exist yet.**
   - Choice A: Describe invisible factors
   - Choice B: Avoid the strange idea
   - Main tension: Curiosity vs Credibility

10. **A visitor wants practical crop advice.**
   - Choice A: Explain inheritance carefully
   - Choice B: Promise better peas
   - Main tension: Credibility vs Cash

11. **The notebooks outlive the excitement.**
   - Choice A: Keep them clear
   - Choice B: Let the details scatter
   - Main tension: Legacy vs Capacity

12. **Future scientists finally recognize the work.**
   - Choice A: Let the peas win late
   - Choice B: Demand applause from history
   - Main tension: Legacy vs Reputation


## Collapse endings to consider

- **Credibility too low:** the field stops trusting the work.
- **Credibility too high:** the scientist becomes trapped by reputation and cannot take honest risks.
- **Curiosity too low:** the path becomes safe, dull, and scientifically stagnant.
- **Curiosity too high:** the scientist chases ideas faster than evidence can support them.
- **Cash too low:** the lab, field site, ship, or project cannot keep operating.
- **Cash too high:** sponsors, prestige, or institutional goals pull the work away from the core question.
- **Capacity too low:** exhaustion, isolation, or overload ends the run.
- **Capacity too high:** the scientist avoids enough risk that the path loses momentum.

## Tone guardrails

- Keep satire aimed at institutions, incentives, committees, funding systems, media pressure, and career absurdity.
- Treat the scientist respectfully.
- Make specific historical claims only when they are covered by the source notes.
- Let funny fictionalized cards express plausible pressure, not invented biography.
